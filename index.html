What it is

A camera reads your face and decides whether it is readable enough to be recognised.

The result is delivered through the seat beneath you. When the system cannot read you, the bench slowly tilts. Access is not refused — it is quietly withdrawn.

Where it comes from

Incheon, 2021 — ≈170 million facial images → private AI, without consent.

Facial images collected at a Korean airport, from foreign nationals and citizens alike, were handed to private companies to train AI — without consent.

Faces taken from some become the standard of readable for everyone, contestable by no one. The work calls this extractive legibility.

The standard is one face

This system measures you against a single enrolled face — the artist's own. “Readability” is not neutral; it is anchored to one specific body.

Most people are read as unreadable.

What it asks

Recognition here is not about identity. It asks whether you can be read — and turns that into permission.

Some visitors are shown their score; others only feel the bench. Both adjust, trying to raise it. Neither can — the standard never moves. The same logic now runs through borders, welfare, banking, and digital ID.

Your data

No facial images or biometric data are stored. The system keeps only a recognition score, coarse age/gender/expression estimates, posture behaviour, and an optional survey answer — for academic research only.
